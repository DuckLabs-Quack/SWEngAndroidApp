First steps

Finance

Timesheets

Initially, our group took a somewhat relaxed approach to the timesheets for this module. However, after a week or two a group member began to manage the timesheets (Finance Manager), and a structure was put in place (which was perfected over the course of the module):

  • A weekly deadline was quickly implemented-the timesheets for a particular week had to be completed and available on the Google Drive before 5pm on the Wednesday of the following week, and for the first few weeks of this system the group was notified on the messenger service we used to discuss anything in relation to the module (Slack) twice on the day.
  • This way, the group members had a few days to fill their timesheet in while providing the Finance Manager with sufficient time to conduct analysis and create the Financial Reports.
  • Every Wednesday evening the Finance Manager checked over everyone's timesheet to ensure that the entered information was correct and accurate, and that no information or timesheet was missing.
  • If a timesheet happened to be filled in incorrectly, or was missing alltogether, after the deadline, the Financial Manager gave out warnings for the first two weeks before implementing a discplinary practice: if the timesheet was filled in incorrectly or was missing, then the hours and wages for that person were not going to be recorded, therefore reducing the number of incomplete or missing timesheets to almost none in the following weeks.
  • In order for every member to fully understand, I explained how the timesheet templates worked in one of the group meetings, and answered any questions asked over the duration of the module.
  • From 07/05/18 onwards, this system was handed over to one of the newly appointed Deputy Finance Managers (the Timesheet Manager), while the Finance Manager oversaw the process and had final say on the recording of the hours provided in the timesheets.

Over the process of this module, two timesheet templates were created (the templates are explained in further detail in the Finance section of the tour), and the final full timesheet was for the week commencing 28/5/18 (an incomplete timesheet for 4/6/18 has also been included).

Financial Business Plan

[09/02/18-22/02/18]

The Process

The Financial Business Plan was prepared by the Finance Manager, using the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS), Gannt Chart, and other documents related to the forecast hours our group would work given by the Project Manager (from which forecast wages were calculated for the Cash Flow Forecast). The Finance Manager chose to calculate the wages using direct labour hours rather than assuming that group members would work for 8 hours per day, following discussion with the Financial Adviser, so that the group was only being paid for work done, which would reduce the risk of the loan requested being far greater than what was actually needed.

The Business Plan included a Cash Flow Forecast, calculations of overheads, interest and wages, stated the relevant assumptions, and provided our initial loan request, Â£31,000. While the plan was submitted on time, and the loan request approved, however, it became apparent within days that the hours had been under-predicted (following research as well as discussion with other groups and the Financial Adviser), and so over the next two weeks multiple versions of the WBS were created, from which there were multiple versions of the Financial Business Plan, the Finance Manager ensuring a temporary Financial Business Plan was created and handed out for the Group Tender Presentation. Eventually the final WBS hours were provided, and the final Financial Business Plan was created in two days (two days before the Financial Report One deadline). In the initial version, the Project Manager decided the forecast hours based upon how long it was believed that each task would take. However, over time the method developed into discussing with every group member how long they foresaw tasks they were either already working on, or eventually would, would be.

Both the initial and final drafts of the Financial Business Plan are available in the Finance section.

Financial reports

Financial Report One

[Reporting Period: 15/01/18 - 18/02/18]

The Process

As previously mentioned, this Financial Report was created within a few days of receiving the final forecast hours in the form of the WBS from the Project Manager, only having a day to complete it after the final Financial Business Plan was submitted. Initially, in order to analyse our efficiency as a group, the Finance Manager outlined that they needed the following documents:

  1. A spreadsheet comparing the actual and forecast hours for each group member, per week, and calculating a percentage difference for the entire group, also per week.
  2. A spreadsheet comparing the actual and forecast hours for each group member, per task, per week, which also indicated which hours were spent on what type of task (Meetings, Documentation (such as the Quality Analysis Manual), Finance, Research, and Development (mostly software-related)), as well as what hours were spent on which deliverables for the actual hours worked (in the form of a bar chart) and another bar chart indicating the adherance of our team to the forecast hours. This document was included in the report submission as an appendix.

These particular documents were chosen following discussion with the Financial Adviser, and because it would enable the Finance Manager to analyse the efficiency of each member of our group, as well as how we were adhering to our forecast wages, a useful indicator for our work potential as larger deadlines, such as the First Iteration. However, as mentioned in the section covering the creation of the WBS, the software used (Microsoft Project) for the creation of the WBS and Gannt Chart distributed the hours in a manner that was undesirable (it would have been preferable for the hours to be greater for the related task as the deadline for the task approached, however the hours for all tasks were evenly distributed throughout the development phase, which resulted in the appearance that our group's actual hours were not being spent on the tasks as forecasted/needed).

Given the proximity of the deadline after the Financial Business Plan final draft submission, the Financial Manager delegated the second of these spreadsheets, which was eventually referred to as the 'Analysis Document' to certain group members (Marco, St.John and Jack at first) to fill in. The Finance Manager oversaw the process while completing the rest of the report, and when this document was complete they read through and corrected the spreadsheet accordingly.When the Finance Manager reviewed the Analysis Document, it became apparent that the hours forecast per person were different to the hours forecast per task type, and following discussion with the Project Manager about this issue, the Finance Manager created another spreadsheet highlighting this discrepency and explaining as such.

As well as the aforementioned documents, the Finance Manager looked into what deadlines they and their fellow members had before writing up week-by-week explanations, highlighting the Microsoft Project software issue as well as explaining the ways in whichthe actual and forecast hours varied, both from a quantitative (ie: total actual hours per role, per week) and qualitative (ie: hours per week per task-type) perspective. They also followed the common accounting practice of outlining where assumptions were made (such as ignoring advertising costs, because the duration of the module only covered the development phase and therefore our product was never going to be launched, a viewpoint confirmed as correct by the Financial Adviser (the Finance Manager had regular meetings with the Financial Adviser in order to ensure that they produced the reports and other work as desired)).

For this document submission, the Finance Manager included Analysis Document as an appendix, however in retrospect they would have included a copy of the Cash Flow Statement as an appendix, which was carried out in the rest of the reports. Also, another change that was made to subsequent reports was that the Cash Flow Statement, that included the forecast and actual values, as well as the variance between them, per week in this report, which the Finance Manager concluded was slightly cumbersome, the method being replace in future reports with a Cash Flow Statement that was a hybrid of actual and forecast values (actual until the final full timesheet was available, forecast for the following weeks), an approach which was particularly efficient, especially with the addition of a section under the Cash Flow Statement comparing the actual and forecast opening and closing balance, as well as the variance inbetween. This approach was preferred in future as it was easier to automatically view how much of our budget was left after entering the actual values.

Financial Report Two

[Reporting Period: 19/02/18 - 04/03/18]

The Process

Learning from approaches taken in Financial Report One, the Finance Manager updated the now hybrid Cash Flow Statement (which was also included as an appendix, along with the Analysis Document) on a weekly basis, which enabled the Finance Manager to provide accurate information about our group's financial position on demand, which was useful during meetings (This practice was implemented because the Cash Flow Statement had previously been created in a rush, with no weekly updates, which, while partly because of the lack of management of timesheets, clearly had the potential to become a significant problem in the future.). In addition, a red line running along the right hand side of the column for the latest week of recorded actual hours worked, which was updated with the rest of the Cash Flow Statement, and enabled the reader to notice the change from forecast to actual values with more ease.

While the reporting period was only over the course of two weeks (during which the first loan payment occured), the Finance Manager took the opportunity of the shorter reporting period to implement these new techniques before reports that would cover longer periods of time (such as Financial Report 3) would require completion. Other than the changes described, the report followed a similar stucture to the last and outlined issues related to the planning software used, again explaining that the hours that were allocated over these weeks for development of our app would be used closer to the deadline, when there was a clearer plan for delegation of user stories, as well as when all user stories for this iteration had been decided

Testing

04/06/2018 - 06/06/2018

Testing was conducted following a Testing and Integration spreadsheet created by the Software manager. This can be found here.

Testing was broken down into 5 test sets, which were to be worked through in order. These sets are as follows; First Iteration, Second iteration (Mandatory), Second iteration (Desirables), Second iteration (Optional) and contract code tests.

To pass a test, the app must be capable of completeing the function required of it as specified in the testing sheet. If a test were to fail, then the testing process would be paused whilst the issue was rectified. Once fixed, the test could be marked as a pass. If an issue could not be fixed for whatever reason, then the test would be marked as a failure, however this did not occur during testing of the Hands Off application. If the criteria for a test was not met and there was no evidence of the code having been developed, the test would be marked as missing. When testing was completed, the tests that were marked as missing, would be reviewed and if possible, added to the project by team members.

Financial Performance Review/Financial Summary Report

The Process:

In preparation for the creation of this report, the Finance Manager worked with the Deputy Finance (Research) Manager to conduct research into areas such as product pricing and sales volumes, which combined with the survey conducted by the Marketing Manager resulted in the Finance Manager deciding the sales volume (pre churn and retention rates) and the product price. In addition, research was conducted into typical costs of renting and owning a server, as well as typical salaries for people working at start-ups, which, combined with the Finance Manager's own research, served as the foundation upon which it was decided the salaries for the three employees working at the Enterprise after the launch of our product.

The Finance Manager also had multiple meetings with the Financial Adivser in order to truly understand how to decide the price for the group's produce, as well as what other ratios they needed to calculate, and asked the Project Manager to create an Executive Summary, which the Finance Manager eventually updated.

Using the information procured through these meetings, as well as group research and information learnt through creation of the Financial Reports, the Finance Manager subsequently created the first draft for the Financial Summary Report, including an updated Cash Flow Statement (so that the most accurate closing balance for the development phase was used) as well as the Profit and Loss Statement and Balance Sheet across the decided reporting period.

The Finance Manager, following discussion with the Financial Adviser, eventually decided to report on three financial periods, one being the development phase (lasting 24 weeks), and the other two being the two years following product launch. This was because of how volatile the app market is, the lack of predictability meaning that the market is very unstable, and therefore it would be best to keep the reporting period short in order to use statistics for predictions such as possible sales volume.

In addition to the aforementioned documents, the Finance Manager carried out a SWOT analysis, decided the company objectives over the decided reporting period (as well as longer-term), calculated the financial analysis ratios they decided were appropriate (such as net present value and burn rate, though the use of full absorption cost + margin was avoided because product pricing should be based upon what the market could tolerate). These ratios, amongst other pieces of information were included in notes sections that accompantied the Cash Flow Statement, Profit and Loss Statement and Balance Sheet.

The research conducted by the Finance Manager and Deputy Finance (Research) Manager was compiled into an appendix (See the Finance and the Research sections), an appendix which also included almost all research conducted by the Finance Manager for the duration of the development phase. Included, along with this appendix, was the updated Cash Flow Statement as another appendix.

However, while the first draft of the Financial Performance Review was submitted on-time, a few days later the Deputy Finance (Research) Manager contacted the Finance Manager, informing them of a clerical error in their research which resulted in the product price being twice what it was, and therefore having a large impact on the report. The Finance Manager re-created the document over the following 10 days, despite ill-health, and took the opportunity to make the following main changes:

  • Additional appendices were included to accompany the document and its current appendices-a document analysing the variance between forecast and actual hours worked per group member on a weekly bases as well as a collection of the references used throughout the report and its appendices.
  • The Finance Manager took the opportunity to also ammend the report according to current churn and retention rates because otherwise our pricing strategy would be wildly inaccurate it would be unlikely that every user who purchased the paid version of the app would continue to use it each year.
